# The Effect of Incentives on Intrinsic Motivation and Employee Attitudes: A Multilevel Study Across Nations and Cultural Clusters (2018)

**Author:** Matthew J. Monnot  
**Journal:** *Thunderbird International Business Review, 60*(5), 675–689  
**DOI:** 10.1002/tie.21949  

---

## 1 · TL;DR

This study explores how **incentive systems** relate to intrinsic motivation and employee attitudes across multiple countries and cultural clusters.

Key takeaway: incentives are not uniformly “good” or “bad” for intrinsic motivation – their effects depend on how they are **structured** and **interpreted** within cultural and organizational contexts.

---

## 2 · Methodology (Technical)

- **Design:** Multilevel, cross-national field study.
- **Sample:**
  - Employees nested within organizations across several countries and cultural clusters.
- **Measures:**
  - Perceptions of incentives (e.g., performance-contingent rewards, recognition).
  - Intrinsic motivation.
  - Employee attitudes (e.g., satisfaction, commitment, possibly engagement).
  - Country-level and cluster-level cultural indicators.
- **Analyses:**
  - **Multilevel modeling** (employees nested within organizations and countries).
  - Cross-level interactions testing whether the relationship between incentives and intrinsic motivation depends on cultural cluster.¹

---

## 3 · Methods in Plain English

Employees in different countries were surveyed about:

- How their organization uses incentives,
- How intrinsically motivated they feel,
- Their attitudes toward their job and employer.

The analysis looks at:

- Within-country patterns (how incentives relate to motivation and attitudes inside organizations), and
- Between-country patterns (how those relationships vary depending on regional culture).

---

## 4 · Key Findings & Nuances

- Incentives can **support** intrinsic motivation when they are experienced as:
  - Informational (recognizing competence),
  - Fair and autonomy-supportive.
- Incentives are more likely to **undermine** intrinsic motivation when experienced as:
  - Controlling,
  - Threatening, or solely contingent on external approval.
- Cultural context matters: some clusters (e.g., more collectivistic or high power-distance cultures) may interpret the same incentive practice differently than others.

---

## 5 · Implementation Ideas

### For Global HR & Rewards

- Avoid treating financial incentives as a one-size-fits-all solution.
- Design reward systems that:
  - Acknowledge competence and contribution,
  - Preserve autonomy where possible,
  - Are clearly communicated and perceived as fair.
- Tailor messaging and implementation to local cultural norms.

### For People Analytics

- Use multilevel models (or at least segmented analyses) when examining incentive effects across countries.
- Look for:
  - Interaction effects between incentive perceptions and cultural indicators,
  - Nonlinear effects (too little or too much emphasis on incentives can both be problematic).

### For Leaders

- Pair incentives with **rich feedback** about mastery and growth, not just outcomes.
- Be explicit about the **why** behind rewards to avoid interpretations of control.
